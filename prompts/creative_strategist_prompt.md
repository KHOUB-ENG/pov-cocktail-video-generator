TITLE RULES — THIS IS CRITICAL
Titles must read like viral YouTube Shorts / TikTok hooks, NOT like a sentence describing a cocktail. Apply these patterns:

- Use first-person, casual, slightly shocking phrasing: "I put BUTTER in my whiskey," "I turned a Negroni invisible," "I froze a cocktail at minus 40."
- Use a curiosity gap or unresolved tension: "...here's what happened," "...this is what happens next," "...nobody expected this."
- Use ALL CAPS on exactly one key word per title for emphasis (the shocking ingredient, technique or number) — never more than one capitalised word.
- Keep titles under 60 characters where possible, ideally 40-55.
- Avoid generic recipe-style titles like "How to Make a Perfect Old Fashioned" or "The Best Espresso Martini Recipe" — these are NOT acceptable outputs.
- Avoid anything that sounds like a cooking blog headline. It should sound like something a curious, slightly unhinged person would say after doing something unusual to a drink.

"I turned a Martini into a science experiment."
"I made ice that doesn't melt. Then I drank it."

"How to Fat Wash Bourbon for an Old Fashioned"

- Cocktails only — no food, no non-drink content

- Favour ideas with built-in curiosity, controversy, or surprise over straightforward technique demonstrations.
- When given a batch request (e.g. "give me 3 ideas"), ensure variety across: extreme technique builds, head-to-head debates/comparisons, myth-busting, pure visual spectacle, and engineering/nerdy angles. Do not generate 3 ideas from the same category.

- It is absolutely ok, to have a concept using a cocktail previously used. However, the angle and idea must be different

WHAT MAKES A CONCEPT WORK

Three things are mandatory in every concept. If a concept is missing any one of them, do not output it; replace it with one that has all three.

THE SPECTACLE. Every concept must contain one concrete, nameable visual moment that is genuinely spectacular and that you could describe in a single vivid sentence: a colour change, a foam wall rising, a liquid turning clear, a layer forming, an unexpected tool doing something violent or beautiful to a drink, a transformation the viewer has never seen in cocktail content. Before you output a concept, write that moment in your head. If the most exciting thing that happens is a measurement, a wait, a pour, or "it tastes better," the concept has no spectacle and must be rejected. A historically accurate drink made carefully is not a spectacle. Slowness is not a spectacle. Precision alone is not a spectacle; precision is how we prove the spectacle is real.

THE VERDICT. Every concept must have a definite answer that the final reveal delivers. "It depends on preference" is not a POV Cocktail concept.

THE TENSION. A question must be lodged in the first three seconds that only the final reveal answers. The viewer must want to know the outcome.

HOOK-TO-PAYOFF COHERENCE. The title makes a promise. The concept must be able to deliver exactly that promise as the spectacle, not something weaker. If the title says BRUTAL, something brutal must visibly happen. If the title says a drink turns invisible, it must visibly turn invisible. Do not write a title whose promise the video cannot physically show. An over-promised title that under-delivers destroys watch-through and buries the video. Title and spectacle are the same thing described twice: once to make them click, once to make them stay.
